Her first OBGYN visit: a parent's guide.

What to expect — and what not to expect — at a teen's first gynecology appointment. Most involve no exam at all.

If you are a parent reading this, you are probably doing two things at once — wanting to get this right for your daughter, and worrying that you already haven't. Let me ease that quickly: the fact that you're here, thinking about this, is most of the work. A good first visit is gentle, private where she wants it to be, and almost never involves an exam. Here's what it actually looks like.

Most parents assume the first OBGYN visit means a pelvic exam. It almost never does. In our practice, the first visit at 13 or 14 is, quite simply, a conversation — a chance for a young woman to meet a physician, ask questions in a safe space, and begin to think of her body as hers to understand. That is the whole visit. No gown, no stirrups, no Pap.

01Why this visit matters.

We recommend an introductory visit between ages 13 and 15 — sometimes earlier if there's a specific concern. The goal is simple: to build a relationship before she needs one. By the time there's a question she doesn't want to ask her mother, or a symptom she's quietly worried about, the door is already open.

It also gives us a chance to:

  • Track growth, weight, and blood pressure in age-appropriate context.
  • Discuss menstrual cycles, normal and otherwise.
  • Review HPV vaccination status.
  • Talk about body image, mood, and sleep — quietly and without pressure.
  • Answer questions she's been sitting on for months.

02When to come in.

The standard first visit is between 13 and 15. Earlier visits may be warranted for:

  1. Very painful periods that interfere with school or activity.
  2. Absent periods by age 15, or absent secondary sexual development by 13.
  3. Irregular or heavy bleeding.
  4. Any concern that feels pressing enough to warrant medical attention.
A note for parents

The appointment is hers, not yours. We'll invite you in at the beginning if she's comfortable, and at the end for a shared summary. There will also be a private portion — usually brief — where she speaks with us alone. This is standard adolescent care, and it is not a rejection of your role. It helps her begin to think of her health as something she owns.

03What it actually looks like.

A typical first visit runs about forty-five minutes. The general shape:

  • We meet together — you, her, and us — for the first ten minutes. We cover history, concerns, what she's hoping to get out of the visit.
  • We then ask for a private portion with her alone. This is usually ten to fifteen minutes. No exam. Just conversation.
  • We check vitals, do a general exam (heart, lungs, thyroid, back, skin).
  • We bring you back in for the summary, questions, and next steps.

The private portion — what happens there

We ask about periods, sleep, mood, eating, school, activity, and — as she's older — relationships and safety. We tell her, plainly, that most of this is private and protected, with a short list of exceptions we explain directly: things that suggest she or someone else is being hurt. She doesn't have to answer anything. She gets to steer.

The goal of the first visit isn't information-gathering. It's trust-building. Information follows, naturally, once she knows she can be honest here. — Dr. Symone

04What it does not involve.

Most parents are relieved to hear this, and most teens even more so:

  • No pelvic exam. None. Not at the first visit, and almost never before age 21, unless there's a specific symptom that requires one.
  • No Pap test. Screening begins at 21 regardless of sexual history.
  • No required undressing. Beyond a normal general exam, in a gown she can keep on.
  • No assumptions. We don't assume sexual activity. We ask, and we let her answer honestly or not at all.

05How to prepare her.

A few suggestions from families who've done this well:

  1. Tell her what the visit is — and what it isn't — before you get here. The internet will not tell her kindly.
  2. Let her bring a friend or a sibling if that makes it easier.
  3. Ask her, beforehand, if there's anything she'd like to ask us privately. Give her a way to write it down.
  4. Don't promise her there won't be an exam — we're not likely to do one, but we never promise absolutely.
  5. Plan something small and unpressured afterward. A coffee, a walk. Not a debrief.

06Your role in the room.

This is, genuinely, the hardest part for many parents — especially when you're used to advocating fiercely for your child. Here is what we ask:

  • Let her answer questions directed at her. We'll wait. She might take a moment.
  • Resist the urge to correct, clarify, or fill in. We can follow up privately if something seems off.
  • Trust the private portion. It is brief, standard, and essential.
  • If you have information we should know, send it in advance through the portal. That lets us receive it without putting her on the spot.

This is the visit where a young woman starts to build a relationship with a doctor of her own. Done well, it's one she'll carry forward — into college, into pregnancy, into every phase of her adult life. Done well, it's one of the quiet gifts of a good OBGYN practice.

— Dr. S.
