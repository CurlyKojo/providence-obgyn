Hormone therapy in 2026: the current evidence.

A plain-language summary of where the guidelines actually stand on HRT — and how we think about it for our patients.

For most of the last twenty years, hormone therapy has been the thing women weren't supposed to take. The 2002 Women's Health Initiative frightened a generation of patients away from a treatment that — used correctly, in the right women, at the right time — remains one of the most effective tools we have for the symptoms of menopause. The evidence has moved. The conversation, quietly, has moved with it.

I want to be careful here. Hormone therapy is not the right answer for every patient, and any honest conversation about it starts by acknowledging that. But for a meaningful number of women in perimenopause and early menopause — especially those with disruptive hot flashes, night sweats, mood changes, or vaginal symptoms — it remains the single most effective treatment we can offer. Most of what I see in clinic is the opposite problem: women who would benefit, but who were told two decades ago that it would give them cancer and have never been offered it since.

01The long shadow of 2002.

The 2002 Women's Health Initiative was a large, important, randomized trial — and it did reveal real risks. But the population it studied was, on average, women in their mid-sixties, more than a decade past menopause. What was read from the trial, and what was reported, was a warning that applied much more narrowly than the headlines suggested.

What we have learned since, through re-analysis and through newer studies:

  • For women under 60, and within ten years of menopause, the benefit-to-risk ratio looks substantially different.
  • The type of hormone matters. Transdermal estrogen (patches, gels) carries different clot and stroke risks than oral.
  • Progestogen choice matters. Micronized progesterone is not identical to synthetic progestins.
  • Duration matters — short courses for symptom control carry less accumulated risk than indefinite use.
The question is almost never "is HRT safe?" — it is "is HRT right for this woman, at this age, with this history, for these symptoms?" — Dr. Symone

02What has actually changed.

The major professional societies — ACOG, the Menopause Society, Endocrine Society — have in the last five years issued clearer, more permissive guidance on hormone therapy for symptomatic women under 60. The language has moved from "consider only when nothing else works" to "a first-line option when symptoms meaningfully affect quality of life."

What hormone therapy is actually good at

  • Vasomotor symptoms — the hot flashes and night sweats that wake you four times a night.
  • Genitourinary symptoms — dryness, painful sex, recurrent urinary infections.
  • Bone density preservation in the critical early post-menopausal years.
  • Mood and sleep, indirectly — often dramatically, once sleep returns.

What it is not

  • A general anti-aging therapy.
  • A substitute for lifestyle work — sleep, exercise, nutrition — which still matter enormously.
  • A decision you should feel rushed into, or embarrassed to question, at any point.
Worth knowing

Local vaginal estrogen is a different conversation entirely. Because absorption is minimal, the systemic risks essentially don't apply — and for women with genitourinary symptoms only, it is remarkably safe, even for many with a breast-cancer history. If you have never been offered it and it might help, ask.

03Who benefits, who does not.

The women most likely to benefit, in my practice:

  1. Under 60, within a decade of menopause, with disruptive symptoms.
  2. Early menopause or primary ovarian insufficiency — where the benefits are especially clear.
  3. Those with bone-density concerns who cannot tolerate other treatments.
  4. Those with genitourinary symptoms, for whom local therapy is often sufficient.

Where we are more cautious:

  1. Personal history of estrogen-sensitive cancer.
  2. Unexplained vaginal bleeding — we investigate first.
  3. Active liver disease, recent clot, or uncontrolled cardiovascular disease.
  4. Patients a decade or more past menopause — we still consider it, but the calculus shifts.

04The risks, in proportion.

Risks are real, and I won't minimize them. But they look different from what most patients were told:

  • Breast cancer: Combined therapy carries a small increased risk, appearing after three to five years of use. Estrogen-only therapy (in women without a uterus) may not increase breast cancer risk at all.
  • Blood clots: Oral estrogen approximately doubles baseline risk. Transdermal does not appear to.
  • Stroke: Small increase with oral; less so with transdermal, and minimal in younger patients.
  • Endometrial cancer: Only a concern if estrogen is given without progestogen in a woman with a uterus — which we do not do.

In absolute numbers, for a woman in her early 50s, these risks remain small. They're comparable to, or smaller than, the risks of a glass of wine a night or several extra pounds. That is not an argument for HRT — it is an argument for keeping the conversation proportionate.

05How we decide together.

When a patient comes in to discuss HRT, here is what the visit actually looks like:

  1. We review symptoms in detail — severity, pattern, effect on life.
  2. We review your full medical and family history.
  3. We discuss non-hormonal options first, including SSRIs, gabapentin, cognitive behavioral therapy, and lifestyle adjustments.
  4. If hormone therapy is appropriate, we discuss type, route, and dose.
  5. We set a follow-up at three months to reassess.

This is not a decision we make in one visit, and it's rarely a decision we make quickly. I'd rather delay starting by a month than rush past a conversation you wanted to have.

06A few honest caveats.

I am not a menopause subspecialist, and for genuinely complex cases — early surgical menopause with complicated histories, or persistent symptoms despite optimized therapy — I refer. What I am is someone who has spent fifteen years watching women be told their symptoms were the price of aging, and who thinks we owe them a better conversation than that.

If you've been dismissed, under-treated, or left unsure — come in. An hour of honest conversation is the first step, and the rest we figure out together.

— Dr. S.
