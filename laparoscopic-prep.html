Trying to conceive: when to seek help.

The usual twelve-month rule isn't a rule for everyone. Here's how age, cycle, and history change the answer.

The question I get most, over and over, from patients who are trying — and quietly worrying — is how long is too long. The standard answer is twelve months. The real answer depends on you. Here is how we think about it at Providence, and the conditions under which the calendar matters less than the phone call.

Let me say something directly, because I think it matters: there is no prize for waiting longer to seek help. The idea that a fertility workup is something you earn by enduring a certain number of disappointing months is a holdover from an older era. Today, a workup is low-risk, highly informative, and often deeply reassuring — and there are good reasons to start earlier than the guideline date in many circumstances.

01The rule of thumb.

The general guideline, from ACOG and ASRM:

  • Under 35, with regular cycles and no known issues: 12 months of trying before a workup.
  • 35–39: 6 months.
  • 40 and over: begin workup right away.

These are starting points, not ceilings. In our practice, we lower the threshold further for anyone with irregular cycles, a history of pelvic surgery, endometriosis, known partner factors, or a strong sense that something is off. "I feel like something is wrong" is, in my experience, correct more often than not.

02Under 35.

For women under 35 with regular cycles, the twelve-month rule makes sense biologically. About 85% of couples trying will conceive within a year, and most of that happens in the first six months. That said, we still welcome patients earlier for:

  • A basic cycle review — are you ovulating predictably?
  • A preconception visit — lifestyle, labs, vitamins, vaccinations.
  • Reassurance about timing, which many couples benefit from.
Small clarity

Ovulation is not always midway through the cycle. The textbook day-14 assumption is often wrong by a week in either direction. If you aren't timing intercourse based on your actual fertile window, we can help you find it — and sometimes that is the entire intervention.

0335 and over.

Age matters — more than most patients are told, and less than the internet suggests. Between 35 and 40, fertility declines gradually but not catastrophically. After 40, the decline is steeper. We shorten the window to six months under 40 and do not wait at all at 40+ because:

  1. The value of early information grows with age — both for success and for decision-making.
  2. Some interventions are more effective when started sooner.
  3. The emotional cost of unnecessary waiting is real, and we can usually save patients months of uncertainty.
The question isn't whether you can wait — most couples can. The question is whether the waiting is giving you information you can't get another way. Usually, it isn't. — Dr. Symone

04When to come in sooner.

Regardless of age, we recommend an early visit if any of the following apply:

  • Irregular, absent, or very long cycles (over 35 days).
  • Known endometriosis or chronic pelvic pain.
  • Prior pelvic or abdominal surgery, including appendectomy with complications.
  • Two or more miscarriages.
  • History of pelvic inflammatory disease or certain STIs.
  • A partner with a known fertility concern.
  • A medical history (thyroid, autoimmune, chemotherapy, autoimmune disease) that could affect fertility.

05What a first workup looks like.

A basic fertility workup is not what many patients imagine. It's mostly a conversation, some blood draws, an ultrasound, and — often — testing for the partner. Over the course of one or two visits we typically cover:

  1. Cycle tracking review — ovulation pattern, timing, luteal phase.
  2. Labs — AMH, FSH, TSH, prolactin, rubella immunity, sometimes more.
  3. Pelvic ultrasound — to assess ovaries, uterus, and antral follicle count.
  4. Hysterosalpingogram (HSG) — a short imaging study of the uterus and tubes.
  5. Semen analysis for the partner — this is 50% of the picture and often the fastest answer.

The whole workup typically takes four to six weeks and gives us a clear starting point. In many cases, the findings are reassuring and we move to timed intercourse or simple interventions. In others, we refer to a reproductive endocrinologist — not because we've given up, but because specialist care is better matched to what we've found.

06A word on waiting.

The patients I worry about most are the ones who wait too long because they feel they should — because the twelve-month rule said so, because a friend took longer, because they don't want to feel impatient. Impatience is not a diagnosis. Information is the right of anyone who wants it.

If you've been trying and you're ready to stop wondering, come in. The visit is unhurried, the workup is manageable, and whatever we find, you'll leave with a plan. That, more than anything, is what most patients are actually looking for.

— Dr. S.
