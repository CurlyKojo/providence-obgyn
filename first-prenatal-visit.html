Preparing for a laparoscopic procedure.

Step-by-step: the week before, the morning of, and the first 72 hours at home. Written from our surgical patients' questions.

Most of what makes a surgery go smoothly happens before you arrive. The physical preparation is modest — a few medication changes, a few hours of fasting, a ride home. The mental preparation is the other half, and patients who feel prepared almost always recover faster. Here is the practical arc of a laparoscopic procedure at our practice, from two weeks out to the week after.

A laparoscopic procedure — whether for endometriosis, an ovarian cyst, a hysterectomy, or a tubal procedure — involves three to four small incisions, each under an inch, and a camera to guide the work. Recovery is much faster than open surgery, and most patients are home the same day. That said, it is still surgery, and the preparation matters.

01Two weeks before.

We'll have a pre-op visit approximately two weeks before the procedure. At that visit we review the specific surgery, discuss anesthesia, and go through the consent in detail. We also start preparing your body:

  • Stop aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, and similar anti-inflammatories 7–10 days before.
  • Stop most herbal supplements — especially fish oil, turmeric, garlic, ginkgo, and vitamin E.
  • Review blood-thinning medications with us if you take any.
  • Schedule your pre-op labs — usually CBC, metabolic panel, and type-and-screen.
  • Confirm your ride home and your support at home for the first 24 hours.

02The week of.

A few small things make a meaningful difference in recovery:

  1. Sleep. The week before is not the time to push yourself. Sleep is how the body banks reserve.
  2. Hydration. Drink generously the three days before — it makes IVs easier and reduces post-op nausea.
  3. Protein. Eat well. This is not the week to diet.
  4. Avoid alcohol for at least 48 hours before, ideally longer.
  5. Set up recovery space. Loose clothing, books, a heating pad, stool softener, easy food. Future-you will be grateful.
A practical tip

Put everything you'll need at waist height. The laparoscopic incisions are small but your abdominal muscles will be sore for several days. Bending, reaching up, or stretching low all require the same muscles. A kitchen that's been reorganized for waist-level access makes the first 72 hours substantially easier.

03The morning of.

Fasting: no food for eight hours before, no clear liquids for two hours before. We'll give you specific timing based on your surgery slot. You may take essential medications (heart, blood pressure, seizure) with a small sip of water — we'll flag which ones.

What to wear and bring

  • Loose, comfortable clothing — a zip-front top is easier than pullover.
  • Flat shoes that slide on and off.
  • ID, insurance card, a short list of current medications.
  • Leave jewelry, contacts, and valuables at home.
  • A phone charger and a small water bottle for afterward.

Emotional prep

Most patients feel some anxiety the morning of, and that is completely ordinary. A few things that help: arrive on time but not early (less waiting), bring one person you trust, do not scroll surgical videos the night before, and remind yourself that the team has done this many, many times. The anesthesia team will meet you, and you'll have a chance to ask last questions.

04At the hospital.

You'll check in, change into a gown, and meet the anesthesia team. We'll place an IV, run through the plan one more time, and walk you to the OR — or wheel you, depending on the hospital. Anesthesia typically begins within minutes of arrival. You'll have no memory of going to sleep.

The procedure itself usually takes 1–2 hours, sometimes longer. You'll wake up in a recovery area, where nurses monitor you for 1–2 hours before you're discharged home. This is normal and not a sign of anything wrong — the pharmacology of waking from anesthesia simply takes time.

Almost every question I get post-operatively is answered by two things: hydration and patience. The body recovers on its own schedule — your job is to stay out of its way. — Dr. Symone

05The first 72 hours.

The three most common surprises, in order of frequency:

  1. Shoulder pain. The carbon dioxide we use to inflate the abdomen irritates the diaphragm. It's surprising, it can be sharp, and it resolves in 24–72 hours. Walk, move, and breathe deeply — these help it dissipate.
  2. Constipation. Anesthesia and pain medication both slow the gut. Stool softener starting the day of surgery prevents most of this.
  3. Fatigue that lasts longer than expected. Most patients feel tired for 5–7 days. Do not schedule anything important that week.

Managing the incisions

  • Keep them clean and dry. Showers are usually fine the day after; baths, pools, and hot tubs wait 2 weeks.
  • Some bruising and minor swelling is normal.
  • Expect to feel a pulling or tugging sensation for several days. This is not a tear — it's healing.
  • Light activity, including short walks, starts the day of surgery. Walking is the single best thing you can do.

What to eat

Start light — broth, toast, crackers — and progress as tolerated. Most patients are back to a normal diet within 24 hours. Hydration matters more than food in the first day.

06When to call us.

Call the office, or the after-hours number, if any of these occur:

  • Fever over 101°F (38.4°C).
  • Worsening — not improving — pain after day two.
  • Redness, warmth, or drainage at an incision site.
  • Chest pain, shortness of breath, or calf pain.
  • Nausea or vomiting that prevents you from keeping liquids down.
  • Heavy bleeding — more than a normal period.
  • Anything that simply doesn't feel right. Your instinct is data; use it.

We'll see you back in the office at one to two weeks for a wound check, and again at six weeks to close out the surgical episode. In between, you have direct access through the portal — use it. No question is too small, and we'd rather you call than wait.

— Dr. S.
