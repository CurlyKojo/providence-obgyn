Your first prenatal visit: what actually happens.

A gentle walk-through of the 60-minute first visit — the labs, the ultrasound, and the conversation most books skip.

The first prenatal visit is the one most books describe in two lines and most practices schedule for fifteen minutes. At Providence, it's an hour — and it needs the whole hour. This is the visit where we meet each other properly, where we set the rhythm of your pregnancy, and where we build the trust everything else rests on. If we rush it, we spend the next nine months catching up.

Most first visits are scheduled between eight and ten weeks, which is when an early ultrasound reads most clearly. If you're calling earlier than that — say, at five or six weeks — we'll usually have you come in sooner for a heartbeat check, and then return at eight or nine for the full intake. No need to white-knuckle a month of waiting alone.

01Before you arrive

A few things we'll ask you to do in the days beforehand — nothing onerous, all of it worth it for the visit to land well:

  1. Complete the new-patient intake packet through the portal. It covers medical, surgical, obstetric, and family history — the kind of detail we'd otherwise spend twenty minutes walking through on the day.
  2. Start a prenatal vitamin with folate or methylfolate. If you haven't yet, don't panic. We'll talk through brands.
  3. Make a short list of questions. Anything you've been quietly worrying about, anything you've read online, anything from a previous pregnancy that didn't sit right. Write them down — the brain gets slippery in exam rooms.
  4. Plan to bring someone if you'd like. A partner, a mother, a friend. It's welcome, and many patients find the first visit easier with another set of ears.
A gentle note

You do not need to fast, drink water to fullness, or wear anything particular. The exam is straightforward and we'll give you privacy to change. If bleeding or cramping prompted the visit, tell the front desk when you arrive — we may adjust the plan.

02The intake conversation.

We begin by sitting down, fully clothed, before any exam. This is deliberate. The conversation is the most important part of the visit, and it shouldn't happen when you're half-undressed and cold. We'll go through your history, your current symptoms, and — importantly — what you want this pregnancy to feel like.

Some of what we cover:

  • Your menstrual history and last period — this is how we confirm dating.
  • Previous pregnancies, outcomes, and anything that was harder than it should have been.
  • Medical and surgical history — thyroid, blood pressure, autoimmune, anything chronic.
  • Medications and supplements, including things you started last week.
  • Family history, both sides, with attention to genetic conditions.
  • Your own questions, fears, and hopes — sometimes these are the most useful answers of all.
The first visit is the visit where a patient stops being a chart and starts being a person. Everything else in the pregnancy flows from that. — Dr. Symone

03The physical exam.

After the conversation, we'll do a physical exam. This is gentle and takes about ten minutes. You'll change into a gown in private, and we'll walk you through each step before it happens — there are no surprises.

What's included

  • Vital signs — blood pressure, weight, pulse. We'll track these at every visit.
  • General exam — heart, lungs, thyroid, abdomen.
  • Breast exam — brief and routine.
  • Pelvic exam and Pap, if one is due. We'll ask first and explain why. Many patients can skip the Pap at this visit if they've had one recently.

You can ask us to pause, skip, or reschedule any part of the exam. That's not a special accommodation — it's the standard. If the pelvic exam feels like more than you want on day one, we do it at the next visit. The care doesn't change.

04Labs & ultrasound.

We'll do an early ultrasound, most often transvaginal for clarity at this stage. We'll confirm the pregnancy, measure for dating, look for a heartbeat, and check for one baby versus two. Most parents find this the emotional center of the visit — we take our time, and you'll leave with a printed image.

The early prenatal panel

We'll draw blood for the standard first-trimester labs. None of these are anything to worry about — they're the baseline picture of your health going into pregnancy:

  • Complete blood count, blood type, and Rh factor.
  • Rubella, varicella, and hepatitis immunity.
  • HIV and syphilis screening — universal, not accusatory.
  • Thyroid function.
  • Urine culture for asymptomatic bacteria.
  • Optionally: cell-free DNA screening (NIPT) at week ten or later.
Our approach

We explain every lab. You will never receive a result without a note from us explaining what it is, what it means, and whether it changes anything. If you're the kind of patient who wants the full numbers — we'll send them. If you're the kind who'd rather hear the summary — we'll do that too.

05What you'll leave with.

Before you go, we'll walk through the next few weeks together. You'll leave with:

  1. A printed ultrasound image and a confirmed due date.
  2. Your estimated visit schedule for the trimester.
  3. Answers to the questions you brought in.
  4. A prescription or recommendation for your prenatal vitamin, if needed.
  5. Clear guidance on what's normal, what's not, and when to call us between visits.
  6. Direct messaging access through the patient portal.

06Questions worth asking.

If you're drawing a blank when we ask "any questions?" — and most people do — here are six starting points patients have found useful:

  • What counts as urgent enough to call after hours?
  • What can I still do — exercise, travel, sushi, coffee?
  • How will we handle a miscarriage if one happens?
  • What screenings are optional, and how do you think about them?
  • Where will I deliver, and with whom?
  • How will this pregnancy fit around my work, my kids, my life?

None of these are silly. All of them are ones we'd rather answer now, unhurried, than in a triage phone call at week thirty-two.

If you have a first visit coming up — at Providence or anywhere — I hope this makes it feel a little less unknown. And if you're reading this because you're deciding where to have that visit, we'd be honored to be the place. Welcome, either way.

— Dr. S.
